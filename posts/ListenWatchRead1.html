I consume an alarming amount of media; more than I care to admit. In an effort that can only be described as selfless heroism, I have decided to curate some of my favorite pieces; both new and old.

LISTEN | The Calendar (99% Invisible)

This podcast episode is a equal parts fascinating and torture. I don't know why I'm so drawn to design that is ripe for improvements that are unlikely to ever be implemented. It could be that I find solace in the flaws of design. Knowing perfection is a destination we will never fully arrive at helps me ease up on my own perfectionism. Or maybe I just like to daydream! In this episode of 99% invisible host, Ben Brock Johnson, explores the serious design flaws of our current calendar, it's origin, vastly improved versions, and why they will never be realized.

Below is one of my favorite candidates to replace the Gregorian calendar we currently use. Fun fact, it was adopted by film/camera company Kodak for a number of years!

"A month is hardly a unit of measurement. It can start on any day of the week and last anywhere from 28 to 31 days. Sometimes a month is four weeks long, sometimes five, sometimes six. You have to buy a new calendar with new dates every single year. It’s a strange design."

READ | Starlink.com

This will be old news for some of you, if it isn't: buckle up and prepare for.. better wifi?

Attempting to find a corner of the earth free of internet connectivity may soon become a great degree more difficult. Love him or hate him, Elon Musk is really pushing the envelope. SpaceX is launching 60 satellites at a time aboard it's Falcon 9 rockets, which are then positioned neatly 550km above the earths surface to beam down memes and TikToks (among other things) to all surface dwellers below.

Of course there are a myriad of unintended consequences that come with adding 1000+ satellites into the earths low orbit. Astronomers have been especially vocal about the obstruction Starlink satellites cause to observatories and casual stargazers alike.

Witnessing a string of these satellites drifting across the night sky helps add credence to concerns of light pollution and obstruction:

SpaceX has committed to painting the satellites with an anti-reflective coating in response. Concerns about reflectivity are good and well, but I'm more interested in the implications of a constantly (and inescapably) connected world. The last 20 years have seen a major shift in human connectivity. There is a part of me that will long for the experience of hiking backcountry without even the sliver of a chance at receiving emails. Another perspective brings excitement at the possibility of remote work becoming accessible just about anywhere and to anyone. Global internet also has huge potential implications for delivery of education and activation of emergency services. To throw another wrench into the whole discussion, how will communist dictatorships deal with the implications of a globally accessible internet?

As per usual, I'm all speculation and no answers. But, I'm excited to see how the world will navigate these advancements.

To see where all the hype is at, check out the Starlink website.
